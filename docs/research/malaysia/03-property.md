## 1. Stamp Duty on Transfer (MOT — Memorandum of Transfer)

### 1.1 Progressive Rates for Malaysian Citizens and Permanent Residents

Stamp duty on the instrument of transfer (MOT) uses a tiered structure applied to the property purchase price:

| Property Value Band | Rate |
|---------------------|------|
| First RM 100,000 | 1% |
| RM 100,001 to RM 500,000 | 2% |
| RM 500,001 to RM 1,000,000 | 3% |
| Above RM 1,000,000 | 4% |

**Worked examples for citizens/PRs:**

| Purchase Price | Stamp Duty | Effective Rate |
|----------------|------------|----------------|
| RM 500,000 | RM 9,000 | 1.80% |
| RM 800,000 | RM 18,000 | 2.25% |
| RM 1,000,000 | RM 24,000 | 2.40% |
| RM 1,500,000 | RM 44,000 | 2.93% |
| RM 2,000,000 | RM 64,000 | 3.20% |

Calculation for RM 500,000: (100,000 × 1%) + (400,000 × 2%) = RM 1,000 + RM 8,000 = RM 9,000.

Sources: [IQI Global Stamp Duty Malaysia 2025](https://iqiglobal.com/blog/stamp-duty-malaysia/), [PropCashflow Stamp Duty Guide 2026](https://propcashflow.my/blog/stamp-duty-malaysia-guide-2026/)

### 1.2 Flat Rate for Non-Citizens (Foreigners)

From 1 January 2026, a flat rate of **8%** applies to all residential property transfers by non-citizens (excluding permanent residents) and foreign-owned companies, regardless of property value.

- **Pre-2026 rate:** 4% flat
- **From 1 January 2026:** 8% flat (Budget 2026 proposal enacted)
- Applies to residential property only; commercial and industrial property use different schedules

**Worked examples for foreigners (from 1 Jan 2026):**

| Purchase Price | Foreigner Duty (8%) | Citizen Duty (tiered) | Difference |
|----------------|---------------------|-----------------------|------------|
| RM 1,000,000 | RM 80,000 | RM 24,000 | RM 56,000 |
| RM 1,500,000 | RM 120,000 | RM 44,000 | RM 76,000 |
| RM 2,000,000 | RM 160,000 | RM 64,000 | RM 96,000 |

Permanent residents (PR holders) pay the citizen tiered rates, not the foreigner flat rate.

Sources: [PropCashflow Foreigner Stamp Duty 8%](https://propcashflow.my/blog/foreigner-stamp-duty-8-percent-malaysia/), [KPMG Budget 2026 Stamp Duty](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/my/pdf/budget2026-tl-stamp-duty.pdf), [Malaysia to Double Stamp Duty for Foreign Buyers 2026](https://alestriaproperty.com/blog/malaysia-to-double-stamp-duty-for-foreign-property-buyers-in-2026)

### 1.3 First-Time Buyer Exemptions

**i-MILIKI scheme (extended through Budget 2026):**

- **Full exemption (100%)** on MOT stamp duty for first residential property priced up to RM 500,000
- Applies to SPAs executed between 1 January 2021 and 31 December 2027
- Eligibility: Malaysian citizens only, first-time property owner, residential property
- Also includes full exemption on the loan agreement stamp duty (see 1.5)

**Previous partial exemption (expired):**
- 75% exemption on properties RM 500,001 to RM 1,000,000 for SPAs executed by 31 December 2023; this tier has not been renewed

Sources: [RinggitPlus Budget 2026 Stamp Duty Exemptions](https://ringgitplus.com/en/blog/budget-2026/budget-2026-stamp-duty-exemptions-extended-for-homebuyers.html), [PropertyGenie First-Time Stamp Duty Exemption](https://www.propertygenie.com.my/insider-guide/stamp-duty-exemption-for-first-time-homebuyers-until-2025-rwgJhrJSM6sqs3vbDvGrvF)

### 1.4 Family Transfer (Love and Affection)

A flat fee of **RM 10** applies to property transfer instruments between immediate family members (spouse, parents, children), replacing the previous variable rate. Announced in Budget 2024.

Source: [ClearTax Stamp Duty Malaysia 2026](https://www.cleartax.com/my/en/stamp-duty-malaysia)

### 1.5 Stamp Duty on Loan Agreements

Stamp duty applies separately to the loan/financing agreement:

- **Standard rate:** 0.5% of total loan quantum (Item 22(1), First Schedule, Stamp Act 1949)
- **Reduced rate:** 0.1% for certain Ringgit loan agreements without security, repayable on demand
- First-time buyers of properties up to RM 500,000 receive full exemption on loan agreement stamp duty (same i-MILIKI scheme)

**Example:** RM 450,000 loan at 0.5% = RM 2,250 loan stamp duty.

Sources: [PropCashflow Loan Agreement Stamp Duty](https://propcashflow.my/blog/stamp-duty-loan-agreement-malaysia/), [IQI Global Stamp Duty](https://iqiglobal.com/blog/stamp-duty-malaysia/)

### 1.6 Payment and Penalties

- Due within 30 days of SPA execution
- Late penalties (effective 1 January 2025): RM 50 or 10% of unpaid duty (whichever higher) within first 3 months; RM 100 or 20% beyond 3 months
- Payment via LHDN's STAMPS electronic system

## 2. Real Property Gains Tax (RPGT)

RPGT is a capital gains tax on property disposal profit. It is the Malaysian equivalent of what Singapore calls Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty conceptually, but structurally very different: RPGT is gains-based and levied on the seller, not an acquisition tax on the buyer.

### 2.1 RPGT Rates by Holding Period (Current, from January 2022)

| Holding Period | Citizens and PRs | Malaysian Companies | Non-Citizens and Foreign Companies |
|----------------|-----------------|--------------------|------------------------------------|
| Year 1 (within 1 year) | 30% | 30% | 30% |
| Year 2 | 30% | 30% | 30% |
| Year 3 | 30% | 30% | 30% |
| Year 4 | 20% | 20% | 30% |
| Year 5 | 15% | 15% | 30% |
| Year 6 onward | **0% (Nil)** | 10% | **10%** |

Key point: Citizens and PRs achieve full exemption (0%) after holding the property for more than 5 complete years. This threshold was set at 5 years from January 2022 onward (previously 6 years under the 2019-2021 schedule).

Sources: [LHDN RPGT Rates (official)](https://www.hasil.gov.my/en/rpgt/real-property-gains-tax-rpgt-rates/), [PropCashflow RPGT Guide 2026](https://propcashflow.my/blog/rpgt-malaysia-guide-2026/)

### 2.2 Chargeable Gain Calculation

RPGT is assessed on the chargeable gain:

```
Chargeable Gain = Disposal Price - Acquisition Price - Allowable Expenditure
```

Allowable expenditure includes: legal fees, stamp duty paid on acquisition, renovation costs (with receipts), agent commissions on disposal, and incidental costs.

### 2.3 Once-in-a-Lifetime Private Residence Exemption

- Available once per lifetime to Malaysian citizens and permanent residents
- Exempts the **entire chargeable gain** on disposal of one private residence, regardless of amount
- A private residence is defined as a building or part of a building used or fit for occupation as a place of residence
- Election must be made in writing on Form CKHT 1A (Lampiran 3) and is **irrevocable**
- Can be claimed in any year and at any holding period (including before 5 years); eliminates RPGT entirely for that one disposal

Sources: [LHDN RPGT Exemption (official)](https://www.hasil.gov.my/en/rpgt/exemption/), [PropCashflow Every RPGT Exemption](https://propcashflow.my/blog/rpgt-exemption-malaysia-guide/)

### 2.4 Automatic RM 10,000 / 10% Exemption

Applies automatically to every individual disposal, even when the once-in-a-lifetime exemption is not used:

- **Exemption amount:** RM 10,000 or 10% of the chargeable gain, whichever is **greater**
- This is a per-disposal exemption (applies each time you sell)
- For partial disposal (part of a property or a share of shares): calculated proportionally using area/shares disposed

Example: If chargeable gain = RM 80,000, the exemption is max(RM 10,000, RM 8,000) = RM 10,000. Taxable gain = RM 70,000.
Example: If chargeable gain = RM 150,000, the exemption is max(RM 10,000, RM 15,000) = RM 15,000. Taxable gain = RM 135,000.

Source: [LHDN RPGT Exemption (official)](https://www.hasil.gov.my/en/rpgt/exemption/)

### 2.5 Other RPGT Exemptions

- **Spousal transfers:** Full RPGT exemption — treated as "no gain, no loss"
- **Immediate family gifts:** Children, parents — treated as "no gain, no loss"
- **Pre-2000 acquisition:** For properties acquired before 1 January 2000, the deemed acquisition price is the market value as at 1 January 2000 (not the original cost)
- **Compulsory acquisition:** No RPGT when government compulsorily acquires the property

### 2.6 RPGT Retention (Withholding by Buyer)

The buyer must retain a portion of the purchase price and remit directly to LHDN within **60 days** of the sale:

| Seller Category | Retention Rate |
|----------------|----------------|
| Malaysian citizen or PR | 3% of purchase price |
| Malaysian company | 5% of purchase price |
| Non-citizen / foreign company | 7% of purchase price |

The retention is credited against the seller's final RPGT liability. If retention exceeds actual RPGT, LHDN issues a refund.

Example: Foreigner sells property for RM 1.5M. Buyer retains RM 105,000 (7%). If actual RPGT liability is RM 80,000 (within year 5, 30% rate), seller receives RM 25,000 refund after filing.

Source: [PropCashflow RPGT Guide 2026](https://propcashflow.my/blog/rpgt-malaysia-guide-2026/)

### 2.7 Self-Assessment System (From 1 January 2025)

RPGT transitioned to a self-assessment system (STS RPGT) effective 1 January 2025. Sellers must now:
1. Self-calculate chargeable gain and RPGT liability
2. File CKHT Return Form (CKHT 1A for sellers, CKHT 2A for buyers)
3. Submit and pay within 90 days of disposal

Sources: [EY Malaysia Updated RPGT Guidelines](https://www.ey.com/en_my/technical/tax-alerts/updated-real-property-gains-tax-rpgt-guidelines), [PwC RPGT](https://www.pwc.com/my/en/publications/mtb/real-property-gains-tax.html)

### 2.8 Budget 2026 RPGT Changes

No changes to RPGT rates or structure in Budget 2026. The existing rate schedule remains in effect.

Source: [PropCashflow Budget 2026 Property Impact](https://propcashflow.my/blog/budget-2026-property-impact-malaysia/)

## 3. Loan-to-Value (LTV) Rules

### 3.1 BNM Guidelines by Property Count

Bank Negara Malaysia regulates maximum LTV via policy circulars. The key rule applies to the **third and subsequent housing loans**:

| Property Loan Sequence | Maximum LTV | Basis |
|------------------------|-------------|-------|
| 1st housing loan | ~90% (market practice) | No BNM cap; individual bank credit policy |
| 2nd housing loan | ~90% (market practice) | No BNM cap; individual bank credit policy |
| 3rd housing loan onward | **70% maximum** | BNM policy (November 2010, still in force) |

**Important counting rule:** The 70% cap applies based on outstanding housing loans recorded in the Central Credit Reference Information System (CCRIS), not total properties owned. Fully settled loans do not count toward this limit. A borrower who has repaid their first two loans entirely can treat the next loan as a "first" loan for LTV purposes.

**For foreigners:** Banks apply additional caution. Typical range is 60-70% for non-residents. MM2H Gold/Platinum holders married to Malaysians may qualify for up to 90% at some banks. No official BNM rule sets a lower cap for foreigners; this is lender discretion.

Sources: [BNM Measures on Property Market and Household Debt](https://www.bnm.gov.my/-/measures-in-promoting-a-stable-and-sustainable-property-market-and-sound-financial-and-debt-management-of-households), [PropCashflow LTV Guide](https://propcashflow.my/blog/loan-margin-financing-property-malaysia/), [PropertyGuru LTV Explained](https://www.propertyguru.com.my/property-guides/loan-to-value-ltv-ratio-for-residential-mortgage-loans-in-malaysia-3601)

### 3.2 Maximum Loan Tenure

- **Standard maximum:** 35 years or until borrower turns 70, **whichever is earlier**
- Example: A 40-year-old borrower can get a maximum 30-year loan (40 + 30 = 70)
- Example: A 35-year-old can get a full 35-year loan
- **Individual bank variation:** Some banks cap at age 65 or 30-year tenure; BNM sets the ceiling but banks may be stricter

**Exception — affordable homes:** BNM has indicated willingness to consider extending tenure up to 40 years for affordable housing segments (below RM 300,000), though not widely implemented.

Sources: [BNM Responsible Lending Guidelines](https://www.bnm.gov.my/-/responsible-lending-guidelines-ensures-borrowers-affordability), [The Edge Malaysia Bank Negara 35-year home loan](https://theedgemalaysia.com/article/bank-negara-35-years-home-loan-period-more-sufficient)

### 3.3 Net Price Rule

Since November 2013, BNM mandates that the LTV ratio is calculated on the **net selling price** (excluding developer rebates, free gifts, interest absorption schemes, or other incentives), not the SPA price. This prevents inflated SPA prices masking the true LTV.

Source: [LoanStreet DIBS and LTV Guide](https://loanstreet.com.my/learning-centre/abolishment-of-dibs-and-ltv-ratio-curbs-in-2014)

### 3.4 Debt Service Ratio

While not an LTV rule, BNM's responsible lending guidelines require banks to assess each borrower's Debt Service Ratio (DSR). Most banks cap the DSR at 60-70% of net income. Foreigners with income outside Malaysia may face stricter DSR assessments.

## 4. Foreign Ownership Rules

### 4.1 Minimum Purchase Price by State

Foreign nationals (non-citizens, excluding permanent residents) must purchase above state-set minimum thresholds. These vary significantly by state and by property type (strata vs landed):

| State / Territory | Strata Minimum | Landed Minimum | Notes |
|-------------------|---------------|----------------|-------|
| Kuala Lumpur (Federal) | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | EPU consent required |
| Putrajaya (Federal) | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Limited stock |
| Labuan (Federal) | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Lowest consent fees (~RM 5K) |
| Selangor Zone 1 & 2 | RM 2,000,000 | RM 2,000,000* | *Landed strata only (gated/guarded) |
| Selangor Zone 3 | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000* | *Landed strata only |
| Penang Island | RM 1,000,000 | RM 3,000,000 | +3% state levy on all transactions |
| Penang Mainland | RM 500,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Significantly lower threshold |
| Johor | RM 1,000,000 | RM 2,000,000 | Strong Singapore buyer demand |
| Perak | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Revised up from RM 500K |
| Negeri Sembilan | RM 600,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Customary land off-limits |
| Melaka | RM 500,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Lower strata threshold |
| Kedah Mainland | RM 600,000 | RM 600,000 | Lowest uniform threshold |
| Kedah Langkawi | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Higher island threshold |
| Pahang | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Cameron Highlands popular |
| Terengganu | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Significant Malay Reserve land |
| Kelantan | RM 1,000,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Most land is Malay Reserve |
| Perlis | RM 500,000 | RM 500,000 | Smallest state, limited stock |
| Sabah | RM 600,000 | RM 1,000,000 | Separate state ministry approval needed |
| Sarawak | RM 500,000 | RM 500,000 | LCDA approval; lowest thresholds |

**Selangor restriction:** Foreigners cannot purchase individual-title landed properties in Selangor; only strata and gated/guarded landed strata qualify.

**Consent fees:** Federal territories: RM 3,000-12,000; Peninsular states: RM 5,000-35,000; East Malaysia: RM 15,000-25,000.

Source: [PropCashflow Minimum Price by State](https://propcashflow.my/blog/minimum-price-foreigner-property-by-state/)

### 4.2 Restricted Property Types

Foreigners absolutely cannot purchase:
- **Malay Reserve Land (Tanah Rizab Melayu):** Land designated exclusively for ethnic Malays and Bumiputera groups under the Malay Reservation Enactment. This restriction applies to all non-Bumiputera including Malaysian non-Malay citizens, not just foreigners.
- **Bumiputera quota units:** Properties within the Bumiputera allocation of a development cannot be sold to non-Bumiputera buyers, including foreigners.
- **Agricultural land:** Foreigners cannot purchase agricultural land unless converted to commercial/residential use.
- **Properties below minimum price:** Attempting to purchase below the state minimum results in EPU consent rejection.

Due diligence: Always instruct a Malaysian lawyer to conduct a land title search before signing any SPA. Cost: RM 50-100, taking a few days.

Sources: [Land Titles and Restrictions for Foreign Buyers](https://professionalharvest.com/land-titles-restrictions-approvals-what-foreign-buyers-need-to-know/), [iPropery Foreigners Buying Guide](https://www.iproperty.com.my/guides/foreigners-buying-property-malaysia-complete-guide-12332)

### 4.3 MM2H (Malaysia My Second Home) Implications

MM2H was restructured in 2024 into a three-tier model with mandatory property purchase requirements:

| Tier | Fixed Deposit Required | Property Purchase Required | Minimum Property Value | Hold Duration |
|------|----------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Silver | RM 500,000 | Yes (within 1 year of visa endorsement) | RM 600,000 | 10 years |
| Gold | RM 2,000,000 | Yes (within 1 year) | RM 1,000,000 | 10 years |
| Platinum | Higher requirements | Yes (within 1 year) | Higher | 10 years |

MM2H holders are still subject to state minimum price thresholds and pay the foreigner stamp duty rate. No special stamp duty exemptions exist for MM2H holders. The property cannot be sold within 10 years of the visa endorsement (the mandatory hold period).

Sources: [MM2H Program](https://mm2h.com/buying-property/), [OwnPropertyAbroad MM2H Guide](https://ownpropertyabroad.com/malaysia/my-second-home-mm2h-malaysia-guide/), [Hudson McKenzie MM2H 2025](https://www.hudsonmckenzie.com/insights/malaysia-my-second-home-mm2h-2025-new-categories-requirements)

### 4.4 Foreign Buyer Additional Costs Summary

Beyond the minimum price and 8% stamp duty, foreign buyers incur:
- State consent fees: RM 5,000-35,000 (varies by state)
- Penang state levy: approximately 1% (Penang-specific, on top of stamp duty)
- Lower LTV: 60-70% vs 90% for citizens, requiring substantially larger cash down payments
- Higher legal fees: Malaysian lawyer required; costs similar to citizen transactions
- No access to EPF housing withdrawals (EPF is for Malaysian workers only)
- RPGT retention: buyer must withhold 7% of purchase price for non-citizen sellers

## 5. Leasehold vs Freehold

### 5.1 Common Lease Terms in Malaysia

- **99 years:** The dominant leasehold tenure; granted by state authorities, typical for most residential developments
- **999 years:** Functionally equivalent to freehold; commands similar prices to freehold
- **66 years:** Some older developments, particularly in certain states
- **60 years:** Found in some states and older grants
- **30 years:** Short leases, now rarely granted for residential; primarily commercial

There is no single national standard; lease terms are state authority decisions.

Sources: [PropertyGuru Freehold vs Leasehold](https://www.propertyguru.com.my/property-guides/freehold-and-leasehold-titles-734), [PropCashflow Freehold vs Leasehold](https://propcashflow.my/blog/freehold-vs-leasehold-malaysia/)

### 5.2 Market Discount for Leasehold vs Freehold

Leasehold properties typically trade at a **10-20% discount** to comparable freehold properties:

- In areas where leasehold dominates (e.g., Penang Island, parts of KL): discount narrows to 5-10%
- In mixed-tenure zones (Klang Valley suburban): discount can be 15-25%
- 999-year leases: minimal discount (0-5% vs freehold)

Bank financing discounts by remaining lease (market practice, not BNM regulation):

| Remaining Lease | Market Price Discount vs Freehold | Typical Max Financing |
|-----------------|----------------------------------|-----------------------|
| 70+ years | 0-5% | 80-90% |
| 60-70 years | 5-15% | 80-90% |
| 50-60 years | 15-25% | 70-80% |
| 30-50 years | 25-40% | 60-70% |
| Below 30 years | 40%+ | Cash only or very limited |

When remaining lease falls below ~30 years, the buyer pool shrinks dramatically and financing becomes difficult, making the property difficult to sell.

Source: [PropCashflow Freehold vs Leasehold](https://propcashflow.my/blog/freehold-vs-leasehold-malaysia/)

### 5.3 Leasehold Extension Process and Costs

Extension requires application to the state Land Office (Pejabat Tanah) under Section 76 of the National Land Code. Key characteristics:

- **Not a right:** Extension is at the **discretion of the state authority**; not guaranteed
- **Timeline:** 1-5 years from submission to new title issuance
- **Cost:** RM 3,000-60,000+ depending on state, property value, land area, and extension duration. Premium formulas vary by state:
  - Selangor formula: Premium = (1/4) × (1/100) × market value per sq ft × years × area in sq ft (with 30% rebate option for upfront full payment)
  - Kuala Lumpur formula: (1/4) × (land use category rate) × land value × (1/99) × (new term minus remaining term)
- **Strata properties:** Extension must be applied for by the Management Corporation or Joint Management Body via unanimous resolution; individual unit owners cannot apply independently
- **One-time scheme:** In some cases, state authorities have offered low-cost extensions (as low as RM 1,000) under special schemes for owner-occupied residences

Sources: [iProperty Leasehold Extension](https://www.iproperty.com.my/guides/extend-renew-lease-leasehold-properties-malaysia-75728), [Sambung Nyawa Leasehold Costs](https://www.sambungnyawa.com/blogs/post/costs-and-charges-in-malaysia), [CBD Properties Selangor Extension](https://cbd.my/extension-of-leasehold-land-in-selangor-kuala-lumpur/)

### 5.4 No Official Depreciation Table (Contrast with Singapore's Bala's Table)

**Malaysia does not have an equivalent to Singapore's Bala's Table.**

In Singapore, Bala's Table is an official schedule published by the Singapore Land Authority (SLA) used for computing differential premiums when lifting state land restrictions. It provides a standardized, published formula for leasehold land value as a fraction of freehold value.

In Malaysia:
- No equivalent official published schedule exists
- Banks use internal approximations; a common rule-of-thumb is a straight-line formula: `Adjusted Value = Market Value × (Remaining Lease / 99)`, though other banks use curved depreciation
- Valuers and banks apply judgment-based adjustments; there is no single standardized table
- Some industry observers use a rough approximation: remaining lease of 50 years ≈ 50% of freehold value, but this is not official

**Modeling implication:** When building a leasehold property decay model for Malaysia, use the straight-line approximation (remaining lease / original lease term) as the best available proxy. Flag that Malaysia has no authoritative published table.

Sources: [PropCashflow Freehold vs Leasehold](https://propcashflow.my/blog/freehold-vs-leasehold-malaysia/), [99.co Bala's Curve (Singapore)](https://www.99.co/singapore/insider/balas-curve-leasehold-property-value/)

## 6. Bumiputera Considerations

### 6.1 Bumiputera Discount

Developers are required by state governments to offer a discount to Bumiputera buyers on a portion of units:

- **Typical discount range:** 5-15% off the listed price
- **State variation:** Each state sets its own required discount percentage; examples:
  - Kuala Lumpur: 5%
  - Terengganu: 7.5%
  - Other states: typically 5-10%
- This discount is only for Bumiputera-eligible buyers (ethnic Malays and other Bumiputera groups as defined by the National Land Code)

### 6.2 Bumiputera Lot Quota and Restrictions

- **Quota requirement:** Developers must set aside 30-50% of units in a development as Bumiputera lots (percentage varies by state)
- **Restriction:** Bumiputera lots can only be sold to Bumiputera buyers in the primary market
- **Subsequent sales:** A non-Bumiputera who legitimately acquires a released Bumi lot must obtain state Land Office approval again before selling to another non-Bumiputera

### 6.3 Bumiputera Release Mechanism

Developers may apply to the Land Office to release unsold Bumiputera lots to non-Bumiputera buyers (including foreigners, subject to other restrictions):

- Grounds for release: demonstrated lack of Bumiputera demand after a defined marketing period
- Applicants must pay a fee equivalent to the Bumiputera discount foregone
- Release applications are frequently rejected; success is not guaranteed
- The process is time-consuming and uncertain

### 6.4 Should This Be Modeled in a Financial Planner?

**Recommendation: No, do not model Bumiputera discounts or lot restrictions in the financial planner.**

Reasons:
1. The discount and lot status are linked to the buyer's ethnicity/citizenship, not a universal financial variable
2. Non-Bumiputera users (the primary audience of an app targeting Singapore residents looking at Malaysia) cannot access Bumiputera discounts
3. The release mechanism is too uncertain and discretionary to model reliably
4. Modeling recommendation: display a text note that Bumiputera-lot properties require legal verification of release status before purchase

Sources: [PropertyGuru Bumi Lot Guide](https://www.propertyguru.com.my/property-guides/freehold-leasehold-land-title-bumi-lot-malay-reserved-land-malaysia-6946), [iProperty Bumi Lot Status](https://www.iproperty.com.my/guides/bumi-lot-check-status-reason-different-bumi-quota-73067), [LoanStreet Bumi Land Terms](https://loanstreet.com.my/learning-centre/bumi-land-jargons-explained)

| 2025 (Q3) | ~0.1% year-on-year (slowdown) |
| Long-term average (2000-2024) | ~4-5% nominal per year |

**Real (inflation-adjusted) returns:** Significantly lower. Malaysia's CPI inflation has run at approximately 2.5-3.5% annually, implying real appreciation of approximately 1-2% per year over the long term. In Q3 2025, real prices declined by ~1.39%.

**Modeling recommendation:** Use 3-4% nominal annual appreciation for base case, 1-2% for conservative case, 5-6% for optimistic case.

Sources: [NAPIC Malaysian House Price Index](https://napic2.jpph.gov.my/en/archives/indeks-harga-rumah-malaysia), [Global Property Guide Malaysia Price History](https://www.globalpropertyguide.com/asia/malaysia/price-history), [Trading Economics Malaysia House Price Index](https://tradingeconomics.com/malaysia/housing-index)

### 7.3 Maintenance Fees and Sinking Fund

For strata (condominium/apartment) properties:

**Maintenance fee (monthly, per sq ft):**

| Property Tier | Rate per Sq Ft | Monthly for 1,000 sq ft |
|--------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Budget / Mass market | RM 0.20-0.30 | RM 200-300 |
| Mid-range (suburban Klang Valley) | RM 0.30-0.45 | RM 300-450 |
| Mid-premium (established areas) | RM 0.45-0.65 | RM 450-650 |
| Premium / Luxury | RM 0.65-1.50+ | RM 650-1,500+ |

**Sinking fund:** Mandatory minimum 10% of maintenance fee under Section 52(3) of the Strata Management Act 2013. Earmarked for major capital expenditures (lift overhaul, roof replacement, external repainting, structural works).

**Practical benchmark:** For cashflow analysis, target properties with maintenance fees of RM 0.20-0.35/sq ft if yield-focused. Above RM 0.35/sq ft materially compresses net yield.

**Worked example (RM 500K condo, 1,000 sq ft, PJ):**
- Quit rent: ~RM 80/year
- Assessment rate: ~RM 960/year (RM 12,000 ARV × 8%)
- Maintenance: ~RM 3,600/year (RM 0.30/sq ft)
- Sinking fund: ~RM 360/year (10% of maintenance)
- **Total annual property costs: ~RM 5,000** (excluding insurance, repairs, vacancy)

Sources: [PropCashflow Maintenance Fee and Sinking Fund](https://propcashflow.my/blog/maintenance-fee-sinking-fund-malaysia/), [PropertyGuru Condo Maintenance Fees](https://www.propertyguru.com.my/property-guides/how-much-to-pay-for-condo-maintenance-fees-57336)

### 7.4 Quit Rent (Cukai Tanah) and Assessment Rate (Cukai Taksiran)

**Quit Rent (annual, state land tax):**

Formula: `Quit Rent = Land Area (sq ft) × Rate per sq ft`

| State | Typical Rate (Residential) | Annual Range |
|-------|--------------------------|--------------|
| Kuala Lumpur | RM 0.05-0.10/sq ft | RM 100-500 |

## 8. EPF for Housing

### 8.1 EPF Account Structure (From May 2024)

Following Budget 2024 restructuring, EPF (KWSP) now operates three accounts:

| Account | Name | Contribution Allocation | Purpose |
|---------|------|------------------------|---------|
| Account 1 | Akaun Persaraan | 75% | Locked for retirement |
| Account 2 | Akaun Sejahtera | 15% | Housing, education, health, hajj |
| Account 3 | Akaun Fleksibel | 10% | Anytime flexible withdrawal |

**Property withdrawals come exclusively from Account 2 (Akaun Sejahtera).** Account 3 cannot be used for housing.

Source: [KWSP Account Restructuring Guide](https://www.kwsp.gov.my/en/w/article/account-restructuring-guide), [KWSP EPF Housing Withdrawal](https://www.kwsp.gov.my/en/w/article/epf-housing-withdrawal)

### 8.2 Six Types of EPF Housing Withdrawals

KWSP provides six withdrawal categories for housing:

**Category 1 — Buy House (Property Purchase or Construction):**
- Purpose: Down payment and purchase costs for residential property
- Maximum: Account 2 balance or (purchase price minus loan amount), whichever is lower
- Example: RM 500,000 property with 90% loan = RM 50,000 maximum withdrawal
- Restriction: Intended for own-occupation, primarily first home. For second and subsequent properties, only Category 2 applies.
- EPF disburses directly to developer/seller/bank (never to personal account)
- Progressive disbursement for under-construction properties (by milestone)

**Category 2 — Loan Reduction (Reduce or Settle Housing Loan):**
- Purpose: Reduce outstanding principal of an existing housing loan
- Maximum: Outstanding loan balance or Account 2 balance, whichever is lower. Minimum: RM 500.
- Frequency: Once per year from the previous withdrawal date
- Available for any residential property in your name, including investment properties and subsequent properties

**Category 3 — Build House:**
- For members constructing their own home on their own land
- Phased withdrawals by construction milestones

**Categories 4-6:** Cover specific cases including government housing scheme assistance, housing loan monthly instalment assistance (emergency), and lease purchase.

**Monthly Instalment Withdrawal (Akaun Sejahtera — emergency only):**
- Temporary facility to assist EPF members facing financial difficulty
- Minimum withdrawal: RM 100/month for minimum 6 months
- Limited to one property per member
- Not available once the house is sold or loan is fully settled

Sources: [KWSP Housing Withdrawal Types](https://www.kwsp.gov.my/en/w/article/epf-housing-withdrawal), [PropCashflow EPF Property Withdrawal](https://propcashflow.my/blog/epf-withdrawal-property-malaysia/), [IQI EPF Account 2 Home](https://iqiglobal.com/blog/withdraw-epf-account-2-buy-house/)

### 8.3 Eligibility Requirements

- Malaysian citizen or permanent resident
- Active EPF member
- Below age 55 at time of application
- Minimum Account 2 balance of RM 500 (for loan reduction)
- Property must be residential, located in Malaysia, registered in the member's name
- Cannot use for commercial property, land without structures, or properties held via companies

### 8.4 Interaction with LTV

EPF housing withdrawals are used to cover the gap between the purchase price and the loan amount — effectively replacing or supplementing cash down payment:

```
Cash needed = Purchase Price - Loan Amount - EPF Withdrawal (up to the gap)
```

For a citizen buying their first home at RM 500,000 with 90% LTV:
- Loan: RM 450,000
- Gap: RM 50,000 (10% down payment)
- EPF Account 2 can cover up to RM 50,000 of this gap, potentially reducing cash outlay to zero

EPF does not change the LTV ratio itself; banks still lend up to their LTV limit. EPF fills the equity gap.

Source: [PropCashflow EPF Property Withdrawal](https://propcashflow.my/blog/epf-withdrawal-property-malaysia/)

### 8.5 Key Difference from Singapore's CPF Housing

| Feature | Malaysia EPF | Singapore CPF |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Account structure | 3 accounts (75/15/10) | 3 accounts (OA/SA/MA) |
| Housing account | Akaun Sejahtera (15%) | Ordinary Account (OA) — 23-37% depending on age |
| Monthly mortgage | Emergency assistance only | Can use OA for monthly instalments (first-use) |
| Down payment | Yes, from Akaun Sejahtera | Yes, from OA |
| Employer match | Yes (12-13%) | Yes (CPF employer contribution) |
| Public housing | N/A (no HDB equivalent) | OA heavily used for HDB |
| Investment properties | Category 2 only (loan reduction) | OA can service private property loans |

## 9. Key Differences from Singapore: Financial Modeling Implications

### 9.1 RPGT vs ABSD: Structural Difference

| Feature | Singapore ABSD | Malaysia RPGT |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| Nature | Acquisition tax — paid when buying | Capital gains tax — paid when selling |
| Basis | On purchase price (not gain) | On realized gain only |
| First property (citizen) | 0% | Not applicable (no acquisition tax) |
| Second property (citizen) | 20% | Not applicable |
| Third+ property (citizen) | 30% | Not applicable |
| On selling (citizen, >5 years) | Not applicable | 0% |
| Foreign buyer acquisition tax | 60% ABSD | None on acquisition; 8% stamp duty only |
| Foreigners selling after 5 years | N/A | 10% of gain |
| Annual holding cost | None | None (no annual property tax on gain) |

**Modeling implication:** In Singapore, property investment is heavily penalized by acquisition stamp duty (ABSD) for investors. In Malaysia, there is no acquisition penalty — the tax is only triggered upon sale and only on the gain. This makes Malaysia structurally more accessible for property investment, with the holding period being the key tax planning variable.

### 9.2 No Bala's Table in Malaysia

Singapore's Bala's Table (issued by SLA) provides a standardized, official leasehold decay schedule that planners and valuers use consistently. Malaysia has no equivalent. Malaysian banks and valuers use approximations (commonly straight-line: remaining years / original tenure), but there is no official published table. Use the straight-line formula as the modeling proxy.

### 9.3 No HDB System

Malaysia has no equivalent to Singapore's HDB (Housing Development Board) system. PR1MA, PPR, and Rumah Mesra Rakyat are Malaysia's affordable housing programs, but they serve a completely different market segment and are not equivalent in scale, integration, or financial impact. These are not modeled.

### 9.4 CPF vs EPF Housing Integration

| Aspect | Singapore CPF (OA) | Malaysia EPF (Akaun Sejahtera) |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Monthly mortgage servicing | Yes, directly from OA | Emergency situations only; not routine |
| Down payment | Yes, from OA | Yes, lump sum from Akaun Sejahtera |
| OA allocation for housing | 23% of wages for under-35 | 15% of contributions (all ages) |
| Housing loan accrued interest | CPF interest must be repaid on sale | No equivalent requirement |
| Impact on retirement | Reduces OA balance needed for retirement | Reduces Akaun Sejahtera; less impact on retirement since Account 1 (75%) is separate |

The CPF housing scheme in Singapore creates a significant link between housing equity and retirement savings (the accrued interest repayment rule). In Malaysia, EPF housing withdrawals from the 15% Akaun Sejahtera have less structural impact on the 75% retirement account.

### 9.5 Transaction Cost Comparison

| Cost | Singapore | Malaysia (Citizen) | Malaysia (Foreigner, from 2026) |
|------|-----------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Transfer stamp duty | 4% (BSD, all) | 1-4% tiered | 8% flat |
| Buyer acquisition penalty | 0-60% ABSD | None | None (RPGT only on sale) |
| Legal fees | ~0.5-1% | ~0.5-1% | ~0.5-1% |
| Agent commission | Buyer: 0%, Seller: ~1% | ~2-3% (split) | ~2-3% |
| Annual property tax | ~10-12% of annual value | RM 200-2,000 (quit rent + assessment) | Same |
| Capital gains on sale | None (no CGT in SG) | RPGT: 0% after 5 years (citizens) | RPGT: 10% after 5 years (foreigners) |
